What is Customer-Centrality?

Customer-centrality is a business strategy based on prioritizing and placing your customers at the center of your operations to deliver a positive experience and forge lasting bonds.

Customer relationship management (CRM) and placing the customer at the center of your organization allow you to gather a wealth of information that provides you with a complete 360-degree perspective of the client. The experience of your customers can then be improved by using this information.

For example:

Client data can be used to learn purchasing patterns, preferences, and engagement.

You can spot chances to produce goods, services, and promotions for your most loyal clients.

Customer lifetime value can be used to categorize customers based on the highest spenders.

According to Deloitte & Touche's research, customer-centric organizations are 60% more lucrative than those that are not, and 64% of those with a CEO who is customer-focused outperform their rivals.

In addition, 90% of businesses only compete based on client satisfaction. Businesses that put their customers first can give customers a satisfying experience throughout their whole journey. Companies must endure a significant change in their organizational structure and culture to achieve this.

3 Methods to Measure the Success of a Customer-Centric Company

The customer success measures used to gauge customer-centrality will vary for every firm. Yet churn rate, Net Promoter Score, and customer lifetime value are the three most crucial customer-centric metrics that need to be closely watched (CLV).

1. Churn Rate

It's getting harder to bring in new clients. As a result, more businesses are spending money on maintaining their current clientele rather than looking for new ones. This is why:

It can cost up to five times as much to get new clients as it does to keep your current ones. A 10% cost reduction has the same impact on profitability as a 2% gain in client retention. Every year, businesses lose about 10% of their consumer base on average (also known as customer churn). Businesses with a high retention rate expand more quickly. Understanding why consumers leave and stay is essential to increasing retention rates. According to Call Miner's Churn Index Report, unplanned moving to a new brand as a result of triggers that could have been prevented costs $35.3 billion. Customers were more inclined to abandon a brand, for instance, if:

Qualified customer service representatives, or "super agents," can attend to customers' needs by attentively hearing their concerns and addressing them before rapidly resolving them.

Consumers weren't feeling rewarded for their loyalty while new customers received onboarding benefits and bonuses because businesses lacked automated self-service channels, such as a knowledge base.

When this information is available, you can compute the churn rate by dividing the total number of customers who have departed in the past year by the average number of all customers (during the same period).

2. Net Promoter Score

Are your clients content? How do you gauge consumer satisfaction?

NPS offers the solution.

The goal of NPS, or Net Promoter Score, is to determine client loyalty by posing just one straightforward question:

Promoters (9-10): These individuals adore your good or service and are inclined to recommend you to prospective customers. Customers that give you a 9 or 10 are loyal and will spend more money with you throughout their lifetime.

Passives (7-8): Those that give you a 7 or 8 are happy with their purchase from you, but they are most likely to switch to a rival if they discover a new or superior product.

Detractors (0-6): These customers are dissatisfied with your goods or services and are likely to harm your brand's reputation by complaining to their friends, relatives, and associates.

Your firm will grow more successfully the more Promoters you have. Indeed, it's quite easy. The NPS is a favorite among company boards and executive committees due to its ease of implementation and measurement.

3. Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)

A business's clientele is its most important "asset" if it cherishes its clients. Customer lifetime value, or CLV, can be used to assess the "health" of long-term partnerships before you spend. CLV determines how much money a customer generates while they are a paying customer. It starts when they make their first purchase and lasts until they choose not to work with you again.

Add together all of your revenue and multiply it by the length of your business partnership to get CLV. Subtract the initial cost of buying them after that.

If a consumer spends $1,000 each year, for instance, and their "lifetime" is typically 10 years, you would increase that amount by 10 to get $10,000. The CLV is now $9,000 after deducting the purchase cost (in this instance, we'll assume $1,000).

Not bad, you'd say?

You can better appreciate why it makes sense to spend on client retention by calculating CLV. But you should consider CLV from a value perspective rather than just a revenue one, which is why I like Dennis Shiao's definition of customer lifetime value.
